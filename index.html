Ignaz Philipp Semmelweis

The man whose idea has been saving a lot of lives

Timeline:

  • 1818

    - Born in Buda (now part of Budapest, Hungary)

  • 1835

    - finishes high school studies and enrolls in a two-year humanities course at the University of Pest

  • 1837

    - begins studying law at the University of Vienna, but by the following year switches to medicine

  • 1844

    - receives his doctor’s degree in Vienna, obtained a diploma in surgery and midwifery

  • 1846

    - is appointed assistant at the obstetric clinic in Vienna

  • 1847

    - discovered that childbed fever was caused by the doctors examining pregnant women without washing their hands after performing autopsy. In the unit where midwives worked (not performing autopsies) childbed fever mortality rate and numbers were not that high. Ordered doctors, medical students and nursing staff to use a solution of chlorinated lime for washing their hands before entering obstetric wards. Also makes chlorine hand washing between examinations of individual patients mandatory. Widened the scope of his washing protocol, to include all instruments coming in contact with patients in labor. Under these procedures, the mortality rates dropped from 18% to 1%.

  • 1848

    - becomes member of the Revolutionary National Guard in Vienna

  • 1849

    - was dropped from his post at the clinic, his superiors did not accept his conclusions

  • 1851

    - takes over the obstetrics department of Rókus Hospital in Pest

  • 1855

    - appointed professor of theoretical and practical obstetrics at the University of Pest

  • 1858

    - published the article entitled

    The Etiology of Childbed Fever

    in the Hungarian Medical Weekly

  • 1860

    - his book entitled

    Die Aetiologie, der Begriff und die Prophylaxis der Kindbettfiebers

    was published in German

  • 1865

    - is locked up in the mental hospital in Vienna where dies of sepsis at the age of 47.

“I think with the greatest admiration of him and his achievement and it fills me with joy that at last he is given the respect due to him.”

-- Joseph Lister, the father of modern antisepsis
